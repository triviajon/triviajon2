oscope64

June 7, 2026 · 3 min read
music projects web

SM64 DS has really good music. i’ve known this the way you know most things from childhood—vaguely, without evidence.

i made oscope64 to produce some evidence.

it’s an interactive oscilloscope visualizer for Super Mario 64 DS music. each song in the game is stored as a collection of individual channels—separate tracks for each voice the composer layered together. oscope64 loads those channels, plays them in sync, and draws a real-time waveform for each one. you can mute and unmute channels while the music plays.

what it actually shows you is how the music is assembled. when you pull a song apart, you realize how much each track relies on everything else. some channels sound like almost nothing on their own—a thin melodic idea, a bass line walking up and down a scale. but together they lock into each other, and suddenly you have the song.

there’s a track called “athletic” with an upper register line i’d been mishearing for twenty years. it blended into everything else and i’d filed it somewhere in the background. isolated, it sounds like a xylophone impersonating a harpsichord.[1] in context, it’s what gives the song its lift. composition is full of choices like that, and the oscilloscope makes some of them visible.

the inspiration came from mr. oscilloscope on YouTube, who does these beautiful visualizations rendered as video. i wanted something where you could actually mess with the channels.

Implementation

the whole thing is vanilla JS with the Web Audio API. no frameworks. the Web Audio API is one of those parts of the web platform that feels like it shouldn’t exist—like real audio signal processing ended up in the browser spec by accident and nobody cleaned it up. you wire together a graph of gain nodes and analyser nodes, decode audio buffers, and suddenly you have a mixing board in the browser.

each channel gets its own gain node (for muting) and analyser node (for waveform data). requestAnimationFrame pulls time-domain samples from the analysers and draws them onto canvas elements. the audio is pre-separated MP3s, compressed with FFmpeg to keep the total payload manageable—there are 85 songs in SM64 DS, each with 11-12 channels, so the math adds up.

if you want to try it, i recommend starting with “athletic,” “mugen,” or “vocal.”[2]
